I have a very clear memory of a trip to a lake back when I was in Paraguay around 7ish years ago. That morning I’d woken up to find the rest of my family gathered downstairs. My cousins were outside playing soccer with each other, weaving around my grandfather’s banana tree while trying to score between the two rocks used as a makeshift goal; the others were scattered along the veranda waiting for the meat to be ready. After lunch, we drove down to the Ypacaraí lake. Back then its waters were pretty murky but they were safe enough to swim in; today that is unfortunately not the case. Every day during recess, my second grade class and I would migrate en masse to the dirt patch between the track and a grove of trees. We'd search for sticks, twigs, and broken bits of plastic. Then, we'd start digging, straight down, into the dry soil. Why? you might ask. I have no idea, but it sure was a fun way to pass the time whenever I wasn't in the mood for blacktop games. As we excavated the dirt patch, a large pit began to form beneath us. We formed dirt canyons, dirt caves, and dirt cliffs. The pit grew and grew, swallowing us whole with it. Change also occurred in unexpected ways. The patch of dirt changed from a dirty russet brown, to a burnt umber, to a blazing red ochre, as we dug deeper and deeper into the soil. We were captivated by the allure of this mystical pit. Some theorized that a colony of "magical ants" had once been living where we were digging, and that they were responsible for this color change. We all thought that this pit was as wide as the ocean: looking back, it probably was no larger than a pizza. Unfortunately, after a few months of vigorous digging, our school decided to embrace "eco-friendliness" and bulldozed the grove of trees, installing an array of solar panels where our dirt cavern once stood. This didn't sit well with us, but we as tiny second graders were irrelevant in the eyes of the administration. We came to accept the fact that our dirt cavern would be buried forever underneath concrete slabs. Yet I still have not forgotten this seemingly meaningless memory and the joy it brought me and my classmates, if only for a short while. (edited)

Prompt 1: When I was 4 years old I committed a murder. It was the day an elegant bottle of aerosolized sunscreen that had tentatively been purchased so I wouldn't burn to a crisp like those rolls my mother had made setting off the kitchen sprinklers. Equipped with this fascinating bottle capable of spraying something that smelled funny and burned my eyes, I proudly doused myself like a baking pan about to enter the oven, leaving a slippery trail and strong odor behind me as I ran into the school flower garden. My best-friend Elizabeth was equally fascinated by this wondrous contraption. "You press here" "MY EYES!" "What happens if I….blech that doesn't taste good" "I don't think we can eat it" "You can eat anything if you want to" She had me there, but we decided it wasn't quite worth it, apparently, it was flammable, and I didn't want to turn into a bonfire just yet. The garden was a beautiful place. It was that time of year when everything bloomed and blossomed. The hydrangeas peaked out in tiny bunches, the pansies bled dyes across their petals, and the daisies smelled like that dust that makes you sneeze. The crown jewel was in the center, the pride of my Spanish teacher, a bright, golden, tall, symmetrical, sunflower. She had been growing this beauty for 2 months, and it now towered over the inferior flora. Of course, the sunscreen was still more fascinating. "So what's it made of?" "Water, I think. It's a liquid" I mean, that's a sensible conclusion right? Juice was made of water and fruit so sunscreen must be made out of water too! Well, I wonder what can be done with water in a garden full of plants that looked a little thirsty? (edited)
Each and every petal was coated until the odor of sunscreen overpowered any trace of floral scent and flower dust. Well of course, why should the flowers get special treatment? The chairs and the tables looked like they could use a wash too. Satisfied, we returned to class, we had saved the garden! Suddenly later in the evening, our teacher rushed in, overcome with shock. Somewhat aggressively dragged out of class after many dots had been connected, we were forced to return to the garden. Oh, what a sight it was! Each and every flower had crumbled and wilted. The hydrangeas had shriveled, the pansies had turned brown, and the daisies didn't smell like sneeze-dust anymore. Our Spanish teacher let out a scream, for the magnificent sunflower had drooped to kiss the worms that looked especially shiny for some unknown reason. The brittle petals lay in a pool at its roots. She attempted desperately to revive it, they tried water, fresh soil, even its favorite smelly fertilizer, but it was too far gone. The news got out for the majestic sunflower was no more, and two second years were responsible! The entire grade mourned as my Spanish teacher carried it tenderly in her arms to her minivan. Three buckets of soapy water, 2 mops, and 5 hours of washing later, we made our amends, but the sunflower was never forgotten, forever immortalized by a newly painted sign that now hangs at the garden entrance "Use of sunscreen in the garden is strictly prohibited!". So we mopped our guilt away, and when the punishment was delivered I smiled at my Spanish teacher and exclaimed "Yay I love washing!". I don't believe she was too sad to see me move the next year. I'm 17 years old. 17 years is a mere fraction compared to man's life on Earth. But, it is an immensely large duration of time in the childhood of a young adult. When asked to write about, 'my favourite childhood experience', what makes the cut isn't the stoic valleys of Ladakh or the serene waters of Kerala or the barren deserts of Rajasthan. What I recollect and hold with utmost reverence, is a habit of the past. I loved to see the stars. For years, I used to sit in the comfort of my room, with the lights turned off, in awe of the stars outside my little window. The aspect that interested me the most wasn't the intricate beauty of the stars in the sky, or the chill winds caressing my face. Undoubtedly, I could've got a better view at the roof. What appealed to me the most, was the sense of peace that it offered. Some may call it escapism, for it certainly was a way out of daily tensions. However, for me it was a retrospection, a journey. I never used this time to escape from my responsibilities, but rather to accept the true weight of it. The vastness and dynamicity of the stars, on one hand often reminded me of the insignificance of a single human life, while on the other hand, made me value my skills, understand my responsibilities and respect the people around me. As the years passed, the habit died a slow death. However, to this day, I sincerely hope my mind treasured those memories. -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- (edited)

Prompt 1: Favorite Childhood Memory For kindergarten, I went to a Montessori school in New Jersey when my parents relocated to the United States temporarily. The country was very new to me, and though I do not realize it now, everyday was a new experience - brimming with curiosity. My parents still talk about how open I was to exploring the country with them; from Fall in Vermont and nights in New York City to long walks in the Grand Canyon and the bliss of watching Shamu in San Diego’s Sea World, when I look back at everything, it does not surprise me that those two years in the States were the most rememberable ever. In fact, I can still picture the birth of my little brother in Princeton University’s hospital while I waited in the hallway. However, the most treasured experience of mine was a day in school. It was around midday and we were given the task of completing a jigsaw puzzle of the world. I do not remember the number of pieces, but it sure was too many. We were told that we would get to spend some time in the park had we completed the puzzle. Everyone but me completed it. Everyone but me left the class to the park. I did not feel a thing until a few minutes later, when a spirit of “I can’t do this” hit me. I cried. I cried because I was left alone with Ms. Szekeres in the classroom while watching my classmates enjoying themselves right outside the window. I cried because Jacques and Druthi were staring and smiling at (or probably mocking) me. I gave up and told Ms. Szekeres that I could not take it anymore and that I wanted to go outside. She smiled, but said nothing else. Though her smile would have seemed ordinary to anyone else, there was something about it that made me want to complete the puzzle. A minute or two later, one big smile was on my face. I did it. (edited)
Well, it did not go as well as expected. They returned. Everyone ran for their water bottles, took a sip or two and sat down. I was standing in front of the door amidst the rushing in and my teacher looked at me with stern sympathy. Druthi laughed at me and I called her a “bad, stupid person” out loud. She did not have to complain to Ms. Szekeres, she was right there. She pulled me over to a corner of the classroom and told me, “You know what you need to do.” I did exactly what I was supposed to do - I took the peace-pebble from a window-sill and went over to her. “Druthi, I am sorry if I hurt your feelings. I would like to make peace with you,” I apologized and handed over the peace-pebble to her as I did so. “Thank you, Revanth. I would like to make peace with you too,” she said, smiling and placing the peace-pebble back in its spot on the window sill. This incident had a deep and long-lasting effect on me. The idea of a peace-pebble to instill the mindset of conflict resolution was something the school succeeded in doing as anyone who had an argument or fought with another person was expected to seek peace with them through the peace-pebble that was kept in each class. Of all the plenty wonderful experiences I have had, this was the first one where I vividly understood an important value for life.

4. Gray It's a good question. Where's gray from? Middle English, obviously. grǣg, as the cool kids say it. But how did it get here? The color, of course, is everywhere--the range of shades between black and white (but without any other colors there to corrupt, as with brown, smh). I personally like it both in heather gray t-shirts and in graphite drawings. Also mourning doves. But I digress. Back to the origins of gray--apparently we can blame Samuel Johnson, the famous 18th century lexicographer, for that. He preferred "gray" to "grey," the spelling more popular in the non-american parts of the world, but most brits didn't care to change their own practice. Every day, I ask myself why. Why did Dr Johnson (as he was often called) feel the need to replace my beloved e with an a? For I am one of those who only write "gray" out of necessity, "grey" alternatively brings me intense levels of peace and joy. Aesthetically, the horizontal line in the e balances upright branch of the r, just as the bulbous curves of the g and sharp diagonals of the y stabilize the word. Why, Dr Johnson, why???
